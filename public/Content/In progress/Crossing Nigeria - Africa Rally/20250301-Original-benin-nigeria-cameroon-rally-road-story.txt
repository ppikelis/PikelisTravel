#Days0–6. Benin → Nigeria → Cameroon 🚗

The West Africa stretch started in Benin, already feeling like locals. It was our second time in Cotonou and the cars were exactly where we left them. Quick air‑conditioning refill and we hit the road again. Rule of the trip stayed simple: fix it when it breaks.

A few hours later we crossed into Nigeria. The difference compared to Benin was immediate — endless markets, traffic everywhere and people everywhere. Between the border and Lagos the road felt like one continuous city.

We passed around 30 checkpoints in 30 km.

Driving further from Lagos to Abuja meant roughly 50 checkpoints between the two cities. Police, drug agencies, traffic officers and occasionally groups that were harder to identify. After a while we developed a simple strategy:
if they had guns — we complied
if not — we kept driving

Surprisingly, we didn’t pay a single dollar.

Abuja felt completely different from Lagos: cleaner, wealthier and more structured. Nearby Zuma Rock made a perfect stop before continuing east.

Then came the big decision — how to enter Cameroon.

North: Boko Haram
South: active rebellion
Middle: terrible roads

Naturally, we chose the middle.

Locals recommended the Mbanga border crossing. Only 50 km. Supposedly fast. Someone online once crossed it on a motorbike. Sounded convincing enough.

It turned out to be the worst road we had ever driven.

After Abuja it took us 17 hours to cover 500 km toward Lafia and Gembu. Where roads existed there were checkpoints. Where checkpoints disappeared the roads disappeared instead. We started at 04:00 and arrived at 01:00. None of the three hotels in town had available beds — so we slept in the cars.

Four hours later we continued toward the border.

First obstacle: a river without a bridge. The only option was to drive through it. Somehow we made it.

Encouraged by the start, we pushed forward another 40 km in about 5 hours. Passports stamped. Photos taken with the village chief. Officially in Cameroon.

Fifty meters later the road disappeared completely.

Washed‑out slopes, deep holes, sharp rocks and sections where only two wheels touched the ground at once. Turning back was impossible. Continuing forward barely seemed possible either.

The numbers say everything: 7 km in 3.5 hours.

At least six times we thought the trip would end there. When we finally cleared the worst section, relief lasted only 500 meters before the next destroyed stretch appeared.

Only then our guide admitted:
“Last time I drove here was about 20 years ago.”

By that point we had already spent 36 hours inside the cars.

Still moving forward.
