Morocco

I have travelled to Morocco 5 times already, and each visit felt different from the previous one. What started as a short trip turned into a series of road journeys across the country, from Atlantic surf towns to high mountain passes and deep into the Sahara.

Some mornings began in Taghazout, walking straight from breakfast to the ocean. It is one of those places where days move slowly and plans rarely stay fixed for long. Other trips led inland toward the High Atlas, including a long push to the summit of Mount Toubkal (4,167 m), the highest point in North Africa.

Further north, Marrakesh always felt like the centre of movement. Markets, gardens, narrow streets and constant noise make it impossible to pass through quickly. Every visit revealed something new, even when returning to the same places.

Along the coast, Casablanca and Agadir showed a different side of the country. Wider boulevards, long beaches and modern districts made them feel more open and easier to navigate than the older inland cities.

Several days were spent far from the coast in desert camps in the Sahara, where travel slows down completely. Nights there felt quieter than anywhere else in Morocco, with long horizons and almost no sense of distance or time.

Somewhere between mountain drives, hammams and long evenings in cafés, Morocco even briefly became a possible wedding destination. In the end we decided against it. The locations were perfect, but the heat felt like too much of a risk for the guests.

After 5 visits, Morocco still feels like a country that is better explored slowly by road. The distances are manageable, the landscapes change constantly, and every trip seems to add another reason to return.
