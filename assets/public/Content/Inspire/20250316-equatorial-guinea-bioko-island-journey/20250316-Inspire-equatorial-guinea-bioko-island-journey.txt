Bioko Island in Equatorial Guinea feels different from most destinations in West Africa.

Spanish influence is still visible in architecture, language, and daily life, and travel across the island remains less straightforward than in neighboring countries. Outside major towns, visiting waterfalls or remote areas can still require permits, which adds an extra layer of planning to even short excursions.

One of the highlights on the island is Pico Basile, the highest point in the country. The road climbs steeply through dense rainforest with almost no switchbacks before reaching a mountain church near the summit that is visited only once per year. From the top, views stretch across the island and toward the Atlantic coastline around Malabo.

Later the route continues toward Sipopo beach, one of the best known coastal areas near the capital. It provides a clear contrast between jungle-covered mountains in the interior and open shoreline along the coast.

In Malabo there is also a small Olympic swimming connection. The city is linked to Eric Moussambani, who became famous during the Sydney 2000 Olympics. Visiting the same pool where he trained makes for a surprisingly memorable stop and a personal comparison over the same distance he once swam.

Travel on Bioko Island combines remote rainforest roads, unusual political geography, and unexpected Olympic history in a place that remains one of the least visited destinations in Africa.
